Rail Intern

Mott MacDonald

In 2026, I completed my first co-op work term, working as an intern at the Mott MacDonald Vancouver branch.

In this role I worked mainly with the freight rail team, but I also did some work with passenger rail near the end of my internship. I worked on many projects, including the high-level design of potential rail facilities, on-site rail crossing inspections, and building simulations for passenger rail lines.

Note: For privacy and legal reasons, the names and locations of projects are not included here. If you think you know the project or location... no you don't. If you work for Mott MacDonald or were the client for any of these projects, and you believe too much information has been disclosed, I can be contacted through my university email (known to MM) or LinkedIn.

The main project I worked on involved the evaluation of multiple sites for possible redevelopment into new train facility. More specifically, to be developed into a unit train loop facility for dry bulk, where a 10 000" (3 km) train pulls fully into a facility, pulls through a dumper to empty it's cargo (while still remaining in the facility), then leaves the way it entered the facility without reversing the direction of motion. As you might imagine, it takes a lot of track to be able to store a full train both before AND after the dumper (minimum 1 train length each side), and trains have a large minimum turning radius, so the footprint of these sites is quite large. For the first part of this project, I designed multiple "generic" options for these loops, in accordance with the rail standards for CPCK and CN and with additional tolerances, and turned them into Civil 3D blocks. We used these block as a preliminary test to see which sites could be redeveloped for this purpose, mainly based on the area on land needed, existing operations that would be impacted, and rail connections. From this I created a slide deck with multiple sites and options to be presented to the client, and they picked out 3 for further analysis. For each of these sites, I then designed a specific layout with respect to the property lines and rail connections and with additional considerations such as possible building footprints, a service road along outbound track, and any infill or structures that would be needed to support the trackwork.

During my internship, I also assisted with 2 site visits. Both of these visits were for the same project, where we would visit select rail crossings to inspect the condition of the rail and of the signage and to check the sightlines for road users, then report on our findings. This work is important as signage are necessary to alert road users to the possiblity of a train crossing the road and how to safely cross and clear sightlines allow road users to see oncoming rail traffic. This work was a follow up to a previous inspection of these sites, to check that the existing sightlines and signage were maintained and that any needed improvements were implemented. On site, I assisted with some measurements and inspection, and activily worked to ensure safe site procedure was being followed (mainly keeping watch for road and rail traffic). After the site visits, I updated all the Civil 3D drawings to show the current sightlines for submission with the final report.

The only work I did for passenger rail was simulation work, mainly using AnyLogic. I worked on 2 models for different light rail transit (LRT) projects. The first project was looking to add a extention and new line to an existing LRT network, with the new line ending at a station shared with the extention. For this project, I modelled the station where these lines would connect, to show how the trains from the new line could interact with the station (drop-off, turn around, pick-up new passengers) while minimizing impact on service for the existing line. The second project involved modelling a full LRT network that was being designed, with multiple variations in trackwork, so that the could check how different combinations of trackwork and train storage locations would impact operations. They wanted to test the combinations to see which could allow for the desired start time and service schedule, as some trackwork would be more difficult and expensive to build. I built the model such that each scenario ran on almost the same code in the same file, where one would switch scenarios by just setting a few relavent variables (ex. brige_exist=true), so that any future changes would be easier to add. For each scenario, trains would be added to the network from their set storage location and begin moving toward their set station to begin service. Trains would begin service opperations once they reached their starting location and once the time was past the starting time, where they would run along their set service line and stop at each station. The software AnyLogic has no built in train signalling, so I manually added processes to check for trains occupying the rail in front of a train and to stop accordingly to prevent crashes. Analysis of the results of this model helped inform the engineers behind this project of possible operational issues for each scenario, so they could better decide which trackwork use with the design. I would like to note that it took me ~3 days to learn to use this software, less than a week to build the model for the first project, then only ~2.5 weeks to complete the model for the second project, which speaks to my ability to learn and pick up skills quickly.

In addition to AnyLogic, I also learned to use RouteRunner, although this was ultimately not used for any of our projects. This program is an Excel add-in that calculates how a specified train would run along a set route (speed along the route compared to max speed and station to station runtime). This program was created by British Rail, and now defuct rail operator, and is largely not used or even known about, which you may notice if you try to look it up. When I was trying to learn how to use it (for a project that fell through), no one in the Vancouver office knew how to use it, and all but 1 of the past projects I was given as reference materials were older than I am. Thankfully, I was able to connect with a colleague at one of the british offices who had used the software before, and we discussed how to use the program over a call. From this call and some practice with the program, I became the branch expert on the program. At the end of my work term I gave a presentation on how and where to use the program to the rest of the office rail team, as well as added pages of additonal instructions and documentation to the user guide (as the original was only 2 pages, with no mention of how what units were used for any value and with very limited instructions). For the project, I had built a python script to take a CSV of rail alignment data (exported from Civil 3D) and output an basic route file that could be used by RouteRunner, which I also created documentation for and added the program to the shared RouteRunner resources.

3D Printing Assistant

Department of Molecular Biology and Biochemistry, Simon Fraser University

In 2025, I worked as a 3D printing assistant. My main responsability was to 3D print models of important molecules, for display and educational purposes, but also to take care of the 3D printer.

When printing models I was not just pushing a 'start' button on a 3D printer. I would general not design models, except for some test models, but I would have to take the provided models and fix any errors that would prevent or make printing worse, cut them up into smaller sections, and update them to print in 2 colours. From there, I would have to communicate with the person who requested the model what specifications they had for the model (colour, size, specific filament...), and slice the model with the appopriate settings. When choosing settings, it was important to have the requested specifications, but it was even more important to choose settings that would ensure a quality print and clean support removal. At this stage I would usually check with them again, especially if the model was particularly large, to ensure it would be printed as they wanted. I would then check that the correct filaments were in the printer, changing them if needed, and print the model. While printing there is not much to do, but I would be sure to check in occationally, to catch any problems if they arose.

After printing, I would remove the print and clean the print bed (if needed). I would then remove the supports from the print, and sand the surface to improve feel and appearance.

I was also responsable for maintenance of the 3D printers. Day to day, this would mostly just be cleaning the print beds (washing off glue), checking for problems while printing, and troubleshooting the problems that occured. I would also have to perform leveling and cleaning, but not often.
